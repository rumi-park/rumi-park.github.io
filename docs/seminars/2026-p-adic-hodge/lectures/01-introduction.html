1 Notations

  • \overline{k}: a separable closure of a field k.
  • \operatorname{Gal}_{k}: absolute Galois group of a field k.

2 Arithmetic perspective

In number theory, one of the main character is \ell-adic Galois representations.

Definition 1 (\ell-adic representation) Let \Gamma be a profinite group. An \ell-adic representation1 is a continuous group homomorphism \rho: \Gamma \to \operatorname{GL}_{n}(\mathbf{Q}_{\ell}).

Example 1 (\ell-adic representations)  

  1. For an abelian variety A over a field k, the rational Tate module V_{\ell}(A) \coloneqq \mathbf{Q}_{\ell} \otimes_{\mathbf{Z}_{\ell}} T_{\ell}(A) is an \ell-adic representation of \operatorname{Gal}_{k}. Here, the (integral) Tate module T_{\ell}(A) is a \mathbf{Z}_{\ell}[\operatorname{Gal}_{k}]-module T_{\ell}(A) \coloneqq \varprojlim_{n} A[\ell^{n}](\overline{k}).

  2. For a proper scheme X over a field k, the étale cohomology H_{\mathrm{ét}}^{n}(X_{\overline{k}}, \mathbf{Q}_{\ell}) is an \ell-adic representation of \operatorname{Gal}_{k}.

Remark. In fact, for an abelian variety A over a field k, we obtain T_{\ell}(A) = H_{\mathrm{ét}}^1(A_{\overline{k}}, \mathbf{Z}_{\ell})^{\vee} as \mathbf{Z}_{\ell}[\operatorname{Gal}_{k}]-modules.

From now on, we focus on local Galois representations. Note that for a global Galois representation \rho: \operatorname{Gal}_{\mathbf{Q}} \to \operatorname{GL}(V), we can induce a local Galois representation \rho_{p}: \operatorname{Gal}_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} \to \operatorname{GL}(V) by composing the restriction map \operatorname{Gal}_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} \to \operatorname{Gal}_{\mathbf{Q}}.2

In number theory, local objects are simpler than global objects. In the local situation taking modulo reduction makes further simplification. Let us see how this happens for Tate modules of elliptic curve.

Let E be an elliptic curve over a local field K, say \mathbf{Q}_{p}. Assume that E has a good reduction, i.e., there is an elliptic curve E_{\mathbf{Z}_{p}} over \operatorname{Spec}(\mathbf{Z}_{p}) whose generic fiber is isomorphic to E. Let E_{\mathbf{F}_{p}} be its special fiber. We want to compare two Tate modules T_{\ell}(E) and T_{\ell}(E_{\mathbf{F}_{p}}). To do so, we need a specialization map.

\operatorname{sp}: T_{\ell}(E) \to T_{\ell}(E_{\mathbf{F}_{p}}). In fact, we construct a map \operatorname{sp}: G(\overline{\mathbf{Q}_{p}}) \to G_{\mathbf{F}_{p}}(\overline{\mathbf{F}_{p}}) for each G = E_{\mathbf{Z}_{p}}[\ell^{n}]. Since G_{\mathbf{Z}_{p}} is proper, by the valuative criterion of properness, we obtain G(\overline{\mathbf{Q}_{p}}) = G_{\mathbf{Z}_{p}}(\overline{\mathbf{Z}_{p}}). We compose this with the reduction map G_{\mathbf{Z}_{p}}(\overline{\mathbf{Z}_{p}}) \to G_{\mathbf{F}_{p}}(\overline{\mathbf{F}_{p}}) to obtain the specialization map. One can show that it is a homomorphism and Galois equivariant with respect to the reduction map \operatorname{Gal}_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} \to \operatorname{Gal}_{\mathbf{F}_{p}}.

If \ell \perp p, then G = E_{\mathbf{Z}_{p}}[\ell^{n}] is finite étale by the following lemma.

Lemma 1 The kernel E_{\mathbf{Z}_{p}}[\ell^{n}] is a finite étale group scheme over \operatorname{Spec}(\mathbf{Z}_{p}) for all n.

In this case, the reduction map becomes a bijection by the following theorem.

Theorem 1 (Stacks Project, Tag 09ZS) For a Henselian local ring R with residue field k, the reduction defines equivalence of categories \mathrm{Fét}(R) \to \mathrm{Fét}(k); \quad T \mapsto T_{k}

Indeed, we obtain G_{\mathbf{Z}_{p}}(\overline{\mathbf{Z}_{p}}) = \varinjlim_{K'/K} \operatorname{Hom}_{\mathbf{Z}_{p}}(\operatorname{Spec}(\mathscr{O}_{K'}), G_{\mathbf{Z}_{p}}) = \varinjlim_{K'/K} \operatorname{Hom}_{\mathbf{F}_{p}}(\operatorname{Spec}(k_{K'}), G_{\mathbf{F}_{p}}) = G_{\mathbf{F}_{p}}(\overline{\mathbf{F}_{p}}).

Therefore, we obtain a Galois equivariant isomorphism of \mathbf{Z}_{\ell}-modules \operatorname{sp}: T_{\ell}(E) \to T_{\ell}(E_{\mathbf{F}_{p}}). In particular, the Tate module T_{\ell}(E) is unramified if it has a good reduction and \ell \perp p. In fact, the unramifiedness of Tate module is equivalent to the elliptic curve E has a good reduction!

Theorem 2 (Néron-Ogg-Shafarevich) Let A be an abelian variety over a local field K with residual characteristic p. The following are equivalent.

  1. A has a good reduction.
  2. For any \ell \perp p, the \ell-adic rational Tate module V_{\ell}(A) is unramified.
  3. For some \ell \perp p, the \ell-adic rational Tate module V_{\ell}(A) is unramified.

The natural question is asking what can we say if \ell = p. For this case, the Tate module T_{\ell}(E) and T_{\ell}(E_{\mathbf{F}_{p}}) are never isomorphic! Indeed, while the rank of T_{\ell}(E) is 2, the rank of T_{\ell}(E_{\mathbf{F}_{p}}) is at most 1 as E_{\mathbf{F}_{p}}[p^{n}] \cong 0, \mathbf{Z}/p^{n} \mathbf{Z}.

Still, there is an answer to this question.

Theorem 3 (Grothendieck) An abelian variety A over a local field K has a good reduction if and only if V_{p}(A) is crystalline.

We will roughly explain about crystalline representations in the following sections.

Spoiler.

  • As its name suggests, we will use crystalline cohomology in place of étale cohomology.
  • The p-adic counterpart of Tate module is p-divisible group. As there is a contravariant relationship between Tate modules and étale cohomology, there is a similar correspondence between p-divisible groups and crystalline cohomology.

3 Geometric perspective

In the previous section, we explained the arithmetic part of p-adic Hodge theory. Still, the question that why it is named with “p-adic Hodge” remains. This section would give an answer.

3.1 Hodge-Tate comparison

The classical Hodge decomposition is as follows.

Theorem 4 (Hodge decomposition) Let X be a smooth proper scheme over \mathbf{C}. There is a functorial3 isomorphism of \mathbf{C}-vector spaces H_{\text{Betti}}^{n}(X, \mathbf{Z}) \otimes_{\mathbf{Z}} \mathbf{C}\cong \bigoplus_{i+j = n} H^{i, j}(X). Here, H^{i, j}(X) = H^{i}(X, \Omega^{j}_{X/\mathbf{C}}) and there is the Hodge symmetry \overline{H^{i,j}} = H^{j, i}. In the other words, H_{\text{Betti}}^n(X, \mathbf{Z}) admits a natural \mathbf{Z}-Hodge structure of weight n.

Tate suggested and Faltings proved a p-adic version of this Hodge decomposition. Of course, the replacement of Betti cohomology is étale cohomology. To state this in the full generality, we introduce the notion, p-adic fields.

Definition 2 (p-adic field) A p-adic field is a characteristic 0 complete discretely valued field with perfect residue field of characteristic p.

Remark. Any p-adic field contains \mathbf{Q}_{p}, the topological closure of \mathbf{Q}.

Example 2 (p-adic fields)  

  1. Finite extensions of \mathbf{Q}_{p} are p-adic fields.
  2. The completed maximal unramified field \breve{K} \coloneqq \widehat{K^{\mathrm{unr}}} of a finite extension K/\mathbf{Q}_{p} is a p-adic field.
  3. For any p-adic field K with residue field k, it contains the “maximal unramified subextension” K_{0} \coloneqq W(k)[\frac{1}{p}]. This field K_{0} is a p-adic field.
  4. The completed algebraic closure \mathbf{C}_{K} \coloneqq \widehat{\overline{K}} of a p-adic field K/\mathbf{Q}_{p} is not a p-adic field. Its valuation group is \mathbf{Q}.

Proposition 1 The completed algebraic closure \mathbf{C}_{K} of K is algebraically closed.

Theorem 5 (Faltings, Hodge-Tate decomposition) Let X be a smooth proper scheme over a p-adic field K. There is a functorial isomorphism H_{\mathrm{ét}}^n(X_{\overline{K}}, \mathbf{Q}_{p}) \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} \mathbf{C}_{K} \cong \bigoplus_{i+j = n} H^{i}(X, \Omega^{j}_{X/K}) \otimes_{K} \mathbf{C}_{K}(-j).

Here, \mathbf{C}_{K}(-j) is the (-j)th Tate twist of \mathbf{C}_{K}. Let us define it.

Definition 3 (Tate module \mathbf{Z}_{p}(1)) The Tate module \mathbf{Z}_{p}(1) is defined as follows: \mathbf{Z}_{p}(1) \coloneqq T_{p}(\mathbf{G}_{m}) \coloneqq \varprojlim_{n} \mathbf{G}_{m}[p^{n}](\overline{K}) = \varprojlim_{n} \mu_{p^{n}}(\overline{K}). The corresponding character \chi: \operatorname{Gal}_{K} \to \mathbf{Z}_{p}^\times is called the p-adic cyclotomic character.

Definition 4 (Tate twist) For a p-adic \operatorname{Gal}_{K}-module M, its nth Tate twist M(n) is M(n) \coloneqq \begin{cases} M \otimes_{\mathbf{Z}_{p}} \mathbf{Z}_{p}(1)^{\otimes n} & n \ge 0 \\ \operatorname{Hom}(\mathbf{Z}_{p}(n), M) & n < 0. \end{cases}

Also note that \mathbf{C}_{K} has a natural \operatorname{Gal}_{K}-action; an element \sigma \in \operatorname{Gal}_{K} acts on \mathbf{C}_{K} by completing the continuous map \sigma: \overline{K} \to \overline{K}.

The Hodge-Tate decomposition can be written in a fancy way using Hodge-Tate period ring.

Definition 5 (Hodge-Tate period ring) The Hodge-Tate period ring B_{\mathrm{HT}} is a (\mathbf{Z}-)graded K-algebra B_{\mathrm{HT}} \coloneqq \bigoplus_{j \in \mathbf{Z}} \mathbf{C}_{K}(j).

Theorem 6 (Hodge-Tate decomposition, period ring version) For any smooth proper X over a p-adic field K, there is a grading respecting functorial isomorphism H_{\mathrm{ét}}^n(X_{\overline{K}}, \mathbf{Q}_{p}) \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} B_{\mathrm{HT}} \cong \left( \bigoplus_{i+j = n} H^{i}(X, \Omega^{j}_{X/K}) \right) \otimes_{K} B_{\mathrm{HT}}.

Remark.

  • The grading on the left-hand side is comes from the grading of B_{\mathrm{HT}}. The grading on the right-hand side is the convolution grading.[^convolution grading]
  • Taking \operatorname{gr}^{0} to the period ring Hodge-Tate decomposition gives the previous Hodge-Tate decomposition.

There is a very important theorem describing the Galois invariant part of B_{\mathrm{HT}}.

Theorem 7 (Tate-Sen) For an integer j, \mathbf{C}_{K}(j)^{\operatorname{Gal}_{K}} = \begin{cases} K & j = 0 \\ 0 & j \neq 0. \end{cases}

This implies B_{\mathrm{HT}}^{\operatorname{Gal}_{K}} = K. Hence, if we take the Galois invariant to the Hodge-Tate decomposition, we obtain a grading respecting isomorphism (H_{\mathrm{ét}}^n(X_{\overline{K}}, \mathbf{Q}_{p}) \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} B_{\mathrm{HT}})^{\operatorname{Gal}_{K}} \cong \bigoplus_{i+j = n} H^{i}(X, \Omega^{j}_{X/K}). That means, étale cohomology remembers Hodge cohomology.

3.2 de Rham comparison

The Hodge-Tate decomposition is not so strong so we cannot obtain many useful properties from this. We need a stronger comparison theorem, and the next step is the de Rham comparison. Let us first recall the classical theory.

For a smooth proper scheme X over \mathbf{C}, the de Rham cohomology H_{\mathrm{dR}}^n(X/\mathbf{C}) admits a natural decreasing filtration, called a Hodge filtration: H_{\mathrm{dR}}^n(X/\mathbf{C}) = \mathrm{Fil}^{0}(H_{\mathrm{dR}}^n(X/\mathbf{C})) \supseteq \mathrm{Fil}^{1}(H_{\mathrm{dR}}^n(X/\mathbf{C})) \supseteq \cdots \supseteq \mathrm{Fil}^{n+1}(H_{\mathrm{dR}}^n(X/\mathbf{C})) = 0. The de Rham comparison theorem is H_{\text{Betti}}^n(X, \mathbf{Z}) \otimes_{\mathbf{Z}} \mathbf{C}\cong H_{\mathrm{dR}}^n(X/\mathbf{C}). This recovers the Hodge decomposition via H^{i, j} = \mathrm{Fil}^{j}(H_{\mathrm{dR}}^n(X/\mathbf{C})) \cap \overline{\mathrm{Fil}^{i}(H_{\mathrm{dR}}^n(X/\mathbf{C}))}. Although this is equivalent to the Hodge decomposition, in the p-adic case, it gives a stronger comparison.

Theorem 8 (Faltings, de Rham comparison) There is a filtered K-algebra B_{\mathrm{dR}} such that, for a smooth proper scheme X over a p-adic field K, there is a natural filtration respecting isomorphism H_{\mathrm{ét}}^n(X_{\overline{K}}, \mathbf{Q}_{p}) \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} B_{\mathrm{dR}} \cong H_{\mathrm{dR}}^n(X/K) \otimes_{K} B_{\mathrm{dR}}.

Remark. The filtration on the left-hand side comes from the grading of B_{\mathrm{dR}} (as there is no filtration on étale cohomology). On the right-hand side, we give the convolution filtration.[^convolution filtration]

We are not going to define B_{\mathrm{dR}}, but let us list the main properties of B_{\mathrm{dR}}.

Proposition 2 (Properties of B_{\mathrm{dR}}) The de Rham period ring B_{\mathrm{dR}} is a filtered K-algebra with a continuous \operatorname{Gal}_{K}-action such that

  • \operatorname{gr}(\mathrm{Fil}^{\bullet}(B_{\mathrm{dR}})) = B_{\mathrm{HT}},
  • B_{\mathrm{dR}}^{\operatorname{Gal}_{K}} = K.

By the first property, we can recover the Hodge-Tate comparison from de Rham comparison by taking the associated graded vector space. By the second property, we can recover the de Rham cohomology from étale cohomology.

3.3 Crystalline comparison theorem

The last comparison theorem is the crystalline comparison.

Theorem 9 (Faltings, crystalline comparison) Let K be a p-adic field with (perfect) residue field k and K_{0} \coloneqq W(k)[\frac{1}{p}] be the maximal unramified subextension. There is a \operatorname{Gal}_{K}-stable K_{0}-subalgebra B_{\mathrm{cris}} of B_{\mathrm{dR}} such that, for a proper smooth X over K with a good reduction X_{W(k)}, we obtain a natural Galois and \varphi equivariant filtration respecting isomorphism H_{\mathrm{ét}}^{n}(X_{\overline{K}}, \mathbf{Q}_{p}) \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} B_{\mathrm{cris}} \cong H_{\mathrm{cris}}^{n}(X_{k}/W(k))[\tfrac{1}{p}] \otimes_{K_{0}} B_{\mathrm{cris}} recovering the de Rham comparison after tensoring B_{\mathrm{dR}}.

Again, we are not going to define the period ring B_{\mathrm{cris}}. Instead, we list some properties of it.

Proposition 3 (Properties of B_{\mathrm{cris}}) B_{\mathrm{cris}} is a \operatorname{Gal}_{K}-stable K_{0}-subalgebra of B_{\mathrm{dR}} with the induced filtration. Additionally, it is equipped with a \varphi-action whose restriction to K_{0} is the Witt vector Frobenius of K_{0}. Also, B_{\mathrm{cris}}^{\operatorname{Gal}_{K}} = K_{0}.

By the last property, we obtain this.

Corollary 1 (H_{\mathrm{ét}}^{n}(X_{\overline{K}}, \mathbf{Q}_{p}) \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} B_{\mathrm{cris}})^{\operatorname{Gal}_{K}} \cong H_{\mathrm{cris}}^{n}(X_{k} / W(k))[\tfrac{1}{p}].

Remark. This can be thought of as “crystalline cohomology is the right p-adic replacement of étale cohomology”. Compare this with the proper-smooth base change theorem for étale cohomology.

Theorem 10 (proper-smooth base change, Milne Theorem 20.4) Let R be a Henselian local domain with fraction field K and residue field k. For any X proper smooth scheme over R, there is a natural map called a (co)specialization \operatorname{sp}: H_{\mathrm{ét}}^{n}(X_{k}, \mathbf{Q}_{\ell}) \to H_{\mathrm{ét}}^{n}(X_{K}, \mathbf{Q}_{\ell}) which is a Galois equivariant isomorphism.

Exercise 1 Prove the (co)specialization map is Galois equivariant with respect to the reduction map \operatorname{Gal}_{K} \to \operatorname{Gal}_{k}. In particular, H_{\mathrm{ét}}^{n}(X_{\overline{K}}, \mathbf{Q}_{\ell}) is unramified if X has a good reduction.

4 Period rings and functors

In this section, we will make a formal setup to express what we explained in a categorical language. Throughout this section, we fix a p-adic field K and let B \in \{B_{\mathrm{HT}}, B_{\mathrm{dR}}, B_{\mathrm{cris}}\}.

Definition 6 (Functor D_{B}) We define a functor D_{B}: \mathrm{Rep}_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}}(\operatorname{Gal}_{K}) \to \mathrm{Vect}^{\ast}_{B} as D_{B}(V) \coloneqq (V \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} B)^{\Gamma}. Here, \mathrm{Vect}^{\ast}_{B} is the category of B^{\operatorname{Gal}_{K}}-vector spaces with additional structure, where the additional structure is given as follows.

  • If B = B_{\mathrm{HT}}, the additional structure is the grading.
  • If B = B_{\mathrm{dR}}, the additional structure is the filtration.
  • If B = B_{\mathrm{cris}}, the additional structure is the filtration with \varphi-action.

Definition 7 (B-admissible representation) We say a representation V \in \mathrm{Rep}_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}}(\operatorname{Gal}_{K}) is B-admissible if the natural map \alpha_{V}: D_{B}(V) \otimes_{B^{\operatorname{Gal}_{K}}} B \to V \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} (B \otimes_{B^{\operatorname{Gal}_{K}}} B) \to V \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} B is an isomorphism. We denote by \mathrm{Rep}_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}}^{B}(\operatorname{Gal}_{K}) the full subcategory of \mathrm{Rep}_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}}(\operatorname{Gal}_{K}) consisting of B-admissible representations.

Example 3 (B_{\mathrm{HT}}-admissibility) Let B = B_{HT}. Then, the map \alpha_{V}: D_{\mathrm{HT}}(V) \otimes_{K} B_{\mathrm{HT}} \to V \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} B_{\mathrm{HT}} is an isomorphism if and only if the \operatorname{gr}^{0}-part \operatorname{gr}^{0}(\alpha_{V}): \bigoplus_{j \in \mathbf{Z}} (V \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} \mathbf{C}_{K}(j))^{\operatorname{Gal}_{K}} \otimes \mathbf{C}_{K}(-j) \to V \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} \mathbf{C}_{K} is an isomorphism as the \operatorname{gr}^{j}-part is the jth Tate twist of \operatorname{gr}^{0}-part.

Lemma 2 (Serre-Tate) The map \operatorname{gr}^{0}(\alpha_{V}) is injective.

Corollary 2 \dim_{K_{0}}(D_{\mathrm{cris}}(V)) \le \dim_{K}(D_{\mathrm{dR}}(V)) \le \dim_{K}(D_{\mathrm{HT}}(V)) \le \dim_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}}(V).

Proof. The last inequality follows from Serre-Tate. To prove the first inequality, we need one fact.

Fact. The natural map B_{\mathrm{cris}} \otimes_{K_{0}} K \to B_{\mathrm{dR}} is injective.

Thus, the natural map V \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} (B_{\mathrm{cris}} \otimes_{K_{0}} K) \to V \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} B_{\mathrm{dR}} is injective, and the \operatorname{Gal}_{K}-invariant (V \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} (B_{\mathrm{cris}} \otimes_{K_{0}} K))^{\operatorname{Gal}_{K}} \to (V \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} B_{\mathrm{dR}})^{\operatorname{Gal}_{K}} is injective as well. The source is D_{\mathrm{cris}}(V) \otimes_{K_{0}} K and the target is D_{\mathrm{dR}}(V). Hence, we obtain \dim_{K_{0}}(D_{\mathrm{cris}}(V)) \le \dim_{K}(D_{\mathrm{dR}}(V)).

The middle inequality follows as \dim_{K}(D_{\mathrm{dR}}(V)) = \dim_{K}(\operatorname{gr}^{\bullet}(D_{\mathrm{dR}}(V))) \le \dim_{K}((\operatorname{gr}^{\bullet}(V \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} B_{\mathrm{dR}}))^{\operatorname{Gal}_{K}}) = \dim_{K}(D_{\mathrm{HT}}(V)) by the left-exactness of \operatorname{Gal}_{K}-invariant.

Remark. In fact, \alpha_{V} and \operatorname{gr}^{0}(\alpha_{V}) being an isomorphism only depends on V \otimes_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}} \mathbf{C}_{K}. Furthermore, D_{\mathrm{HT}}: \mathrm{Rep}_{\mathbf{C}_{K}}(\operatorname{Gal}_{K}) \to \mathrm{Vect}^{\operatorname{Gr}}_{K} is an equivalence of categories with a quasi-inverse D \mapsto \operatorname{gr}^{0}(D \otimes_{K} B_{\mathrm{HT}}) ([Brinon-Conrad, Theorem 2.4.11]).

In fact, there is a more general result.

Theorem 11 The natural map \alpha_{V} is injective. In particular, the map \alpha_{V} is an isomorphism if and only if \dim_{B^{\operatorname{Gal}_{K}}}(D_{B}(V)) = \dim_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}}(V).

Corollary 3 \mathrm{Rep}_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}}^{B_{\mathrm{cris}}}(\operatorname{Gal}_{K}) \subset \mathrm{Rep}_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}}^{B_{\mathrm{dR}}}(\operatorname{Gal}_{K}) \subset \mathrm{Rep}_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}}^{B_{\mathrm{HT}}}(\operatorname{Gal}_{K})

Proof. If V is B_{\mathrm{cris}}-admissible, then \dim_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}}(V) = \dim_{K}(D_{\mathrm{cris}}(V)) \le \dim_{K}(D_{\mathrm{dR}}(V)) \le \dim_{\mathbf{Q}_{p}}(V). Hence, V is B_{\mathrm{dR}}-admissible. Similarly, B_{\mathrm{dR}}-admissibility implies B_{\mathrm{HT}}-admissibility.
